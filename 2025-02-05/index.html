The Imbalance Of Magic in RPGs

Why Punching People is Just Better

As I’ve played through and watched a lot of recent games in the role-playing game genre, I’ve noticed a fairly odd trend concerning magical attacks and how they are balanced; specifically when compared to physical ones . Normally, you’d expect the imbalance to be quite obvious. When we think of magic, we usually think of something powerful. An explosive fireball, a mighty beam of light, spells with the power to rout entire armies of soldiers or monsters. In simple terms, magic is usually seen as incredibly powerful.

But recent JRPG’s such as Metaphor: Refantazio or Persona 3 Reload have made spells significantly less powerful than just physical attacks. This is a common trend when it comes to ATLUS games, as fans point out, but there have been more examples in earlier titles and other series as well.

The Problem Itself

As an example, let’s take a look one of the biggest recent examples first.

In Metaphor Re:Fantazio, the player has access to several classes that their party can switch between, which allow them to have access to powerful skills in combat. But there’s a big difference in power levels that shows it’s head.

The main gameplay loop of turn-based battles in this game revolves around taking down enemies and getting more actions by inflicting weaknesses, similar to the Press Turn system of SMT, or the 1-More system of Persona. Theoretically, magic is incredibly useful for that aspect. And, sure it is. Most of the weaknesses an enemy can have are magical in nature.

And most enemies in the game — with very few exceptions — don’t outright nullify all three types of physical damage, at most just resisting them. That’s necessary. After all, you’d need to be able to beat the game without magic. But physical has several advantages over magic:

  • Physical builds has access to critical hits, and several abilities that make critical hits stronger and more frequent. There are also a significant amount of tools that you can use to boost their power even more; even a class that is specifically designed as a support class for magic gives you a physical boosting skill.
  • Out of the main party, only two of the party members start in magical based Archetypes. Those Archetypes (save for the Summoner) are usually significantly slower to build up than the physical ones, and physical ones come earlier
  • There are several dungeons in the game that force you to not equip magic-using Archetypes like the Healer or Mage. Physical Archetypes have no such limitations.
  • Physical classes get access to elemental attacks that scale off of magic, making magical spells redundant in the late game.

These major factors make it so that for the most part, the best thing you can do in this game is use powerful physical attacks to crush foes, instead of magic. This isn’t to say magic is useless, but it doesn’t come close to the usefulness of stabbing an enemy with a sword.

In other ATLUS RPGs, like the Persona and Shin Megami Tensei series, this imbalance is still present, and in the modern Persona game, there’s an added boon to physical abilities. Resource management. Magic uses SP, which is harder to restore because of the lack of SP restorative items and abilities, and can be used for healing or buffs. Physical, however, uses health, which can not only be built and planned around, but is significantly easier to restore, allowing you to use more physical attacks than magical ones.

I believe that the main issue for these games in particular is that they try to balance physical damage to make up for it’s lacking ability to capitalize on weaknesses, since for the most part, magic is still generally the king in that department. However, they course corrected too hard, tipping the scales to be in physical’s favor.
